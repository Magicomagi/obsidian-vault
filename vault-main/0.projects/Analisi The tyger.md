## Structure and Tone

- ﻿﻿The poem consists of six quatrains in, giving it a pounding, incantatory rhythm that echoes the hammering of a forge.
- ﻿﻿The repeated question, "What immortal hand or eye / Dare frame thy fearful symmetry?" drives the poem's central tension.

## Themes

1. Power and Terror in Creation

- ﻿﻿Unlike the lamb, the tiger embodies ferocity, fire, and destructive potential. Its "fearful symmetry" is both awe-inspiring and terrifying.
- ﻿﻿The imagery of blacksmithing ("hammer," "chain," "anvil," "furnace") suggests that the tiger was forged with great force, unlike the lamb's gentle shaping.

2. Divine Mystery and Paradox

- ﻿﻿The speaker questions how the same creator who made the lamb could also make the tiger.
- ﻿﻿God is no longer simply tender and loving, but a being capable of forging something violent, burning, and potentially evil.

3. Experience and Doubt

- ﻿﻿Unlike "The Lamb", there is no confident answer. The poem is full of unanswered questions.
- ﻿﻿It represents the perspective of experience, where the world is no longer seen as wholly good, but as containing danger, violence, and moral complexity.
